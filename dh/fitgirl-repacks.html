FitGirl repacks

Difficulty: Easy

Every scene has a legend, and in the world of "that game is 90 GB and I have a 100 GB SSD," the legend is FitGirl — a repacker. The job: take a game and squeeze the install down to a fraction of its size, so a 70 GB monster arrives as a ~20 GB download. It is, unapologetically, a compression magic show, and it deserves a page here on the strength of the engineering alone.

Let's be adults about it up top: these are pirated games — cracked copies of software someone else paid engineers to build. This page is a museum plaque, not a menu: no links, no how-to, no shopping list. We're here to gawk at the compression, and to say the obvious — if a game is worth an hour of your CPU screaming, it's worth buying when you can. Developers don't eat compression ratios.

The actual wizardry (this is the fun part)

How do you make a game half its size losslessly, when Steam already zipped it? Games are full of data that's only lightly squashed — audio, textures, video — plus mountains of repetition. The trick is roughly:

MoveWhat it buys
Precompressionundo the game's own weak compression first, so a real compressor can see the raw redundancy underneath
Deduplicationthe same asset appears a hundred times — store it once, reference it everywhere
Heavy LZMA / delta packingthen crush the result with a slow, aggressive compressor tuned for size over speed

The catch is conservation of misery: all that size you didn't download, you pay back in CPU time at install. The bytes have to be reconstructed from their mathematical dust, and your processor does the rebuilding.

The experience, honestly

  • "Selective download" — skip the nine language packs you'll never hear. Genuinely thoughtful.
  • The install bar — a progress meter that has induced more existential dread than any horror game it's unpacking. It says 43%. It has said 43% for eleven minutes. It is lying, or it isn't. You'll find out together.
  • Your fans — achieving liftoff. The running joke writes itself: FitGirl is single-handedly responsible for a generation deciding it was finally time to upgrade the CPU.

So: admire the compression, tip your hat to a genuinely clever pipeline, and remember the plaque. The math is beautiful; the legality is not. Buy the games you love — the fans will thank you, and so will the people who made them.
