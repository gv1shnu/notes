Storage Media

Difficulty: Intermediate

Every storage technology has strengths and weaknesses. Selecting the right medium depends on capacity, speed, reliability, portability, cost and the intended lifespan of the data. There is no single storage device that is ideal for every archive.

Hard Disk Drives (HDDs)

HDDs provide the lowest cost per terabyte and remain the preferred choice for large media collections, backups and cold storage.

  • High capacity.
  • Low cost.
  • Mechanical components are susceptible to wear and shock.

Solid State Drives (SSDs)

SSDs store data in flash memory and provide dramatically faster access times than HDDs. They are well suited to operating systems, active projects and virtual machines.

  • Very fast.
  • No moving parts.
  • Higher cost per terabyte.

A NAS centralizes storage on the local network, allowing multiple devices to access the same archive. Many NAS systems also support RAID, snapshots and automated backups.

Cloud Storage

Cloud services provide convenient synchronization and geographic redundancy. However, cloud synchronization should not be mistaken for a complete backup strategy.

Review version history, encryption options and storage policies before relying solely on cloud services for important data.

Offline Storage

Keeping at least one backup disconnected from everyday systems protects against accidental deletion, ransomware and certain hardware failures.

  • External HDDs.
  • External SSDs.
  • Optical discs.
  • Removable archive drives.

Key Takeaway

Storage should be selected according to the value and purpose of the data. Many well-designed archives combine multiple storage technologies—for example, SSDs for active work, HDDs for bulk storage and cloud or offline media for backups.
