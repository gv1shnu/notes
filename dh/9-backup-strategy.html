Backup Strategy

Difficulty: Intermediate

A backup strategy is not simply copying files onto another drive. An effective backup system answers three questions:

  • How many copies exist?
  • How often are they updated?
  • How quickly can they be restored?

The objective is to survive mistakes, hardware failures, accidental deletion and disasters without permanently losing valuable information.

Recovery Objectives

Before designing backups, determine acceptable data loss and downtime.

QuestionExample
How much data can I lose?One hour, one day, one week?
How quickly must I recover?Immediately or within several days?

A family photo archive and an active software project rarely have the same recovery requirements.

Full, Incremental & Differential

MethodCharacteristics
FullCopies every selected file. Simplest to restore, slowest to create.
IncrementalCopies only changes since the previous backup. Fast and space efficient.
DifferentialCopies changes since the last full backup. Faster restoration than incremental.

Snapshots

A snapshot records the state of a filesystem at a specific point in time. It allows the archive to be viewed or restored exactly as it existed when the snapshot was taken.

Common Mistakes

  • Keeping every backup on one physical drive.
  • Never testing recovery.
  • Backing up corrupted or temporary files unnecessarily.
  • Assuming cloud synchronization replaces backups.
  • Ignoring backup logs and failed jobs.

Key Takeaway

The quality of a backup strategy is measured during recovery, not during creation. Successful archives are built around reliable restoration, version history and regular verification rather than simply accumulating additional copies.
