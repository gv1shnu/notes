Normalization

Difficulty: Medium

ER design removed redundancy by good judgement. Normalization is the same goal made formal — a set of rules that tell you, provably, whether a table still hides duplicated facts.

Problem: Picture one wide table holding players and their team details.

player_idusernameteam_idteam_nameteam_region
1TenZ1SentinelsNA
2Zekken1SentinelsNA
5f0rsakeN3PRXAPAC

"Sentinels, NA" moves to a single row in teams. Rename the team once, and every player referencing team_id 1 sees the change — the update anomaly is gone by construction.

"Sentinels, NA" is written once per player. That single duplication causes three anomalies:

  • Update anomaly: rename Sentinels and you must edit every player's row; miss one and the data now disagrees with itself — the exact inconsistency from the intro.
  • Insertion anomaly: you can't record a brand-new team until it has at least one player to hang the row on.
  • Deletion anomaly: delete a team's last player and the team's name and region vanish with them.

Solution: Split the table so every fact lives in exactly one place. The split is guided by functional dependencies and measured by normal forms.

Functional dependencies

A functional dependency X → Y means: if you know X, you know Y. In the table above, player_id → username and team_id → team_name, team_region. That second dependency — a non-key column determining other columns — is the redundancy, written in algebra. Normalization is mostly the act of giving every dependency its own table.

First Normal Form (1NF)

Every value is atomic — no lists, no repeating groups inside one cell. A past_teams column holding "Sentinels, Cloud9" breaks 1NF; you split it into separate rows (and usually a separate table). The fix: one value per cell, one fact per row.

Second Normal Form (2NF)

1NF, plus no partial dependency — a non-key column may not depend on only part of a composite key. This only ever applies when the key is composite.

Consider match_roster(match_id, player_id, kills, username) keyed on (match_id, player_id). kills needs both halves of the key, but username depends on player_id alone — a partial dependency. Move username out to the players table, and 2NF is restored.

Third Normal Form (3NF)

2NF, plus no transitive dependency — a non-key column may not depend on another non-key column. In players(player_id, team_id, team_region), the chain is player_id → team_id → team_region: region depends on the team, not directly on the player. Move team_region to teams, where team_id is the key, and the transitive link is gone.

The classic one-line summary of all three: every non-key column must depend on the key, the whole key, and nothing but the key. Atomic (1NF) → the whole key (2NF) → nothing but the key (3NF).

Boyce-Codd Normal Form (BCNF)

A stricter 3NF: every determinant must be a candidate key. 3NF can still allow a non-key column to determine part of a key when candidate keys overlap; BCNF closes that gap. In practice, reaching 3NF handles almost everything, and BCNF is the edge case you fix when a non-prime attribute turns out to determine a prime one. Most production schemas aim for 3NF or BCNF and stop there.

Denormalization

Normalization optimises for correctness and writes, but every fact in one place means reassembling it with joins on every read. When a read-heavy query joins five tables a million times a second, you may deliberately put redundancy back — store a copy of team_name next to the player, or keep a precomputed summary table.

This is the Repeat in our loop: denormalization is a conscious trade, faster reads bought with the duty to keep the copies in sync. You only earn the right to denormalize once you understand exactly which anomaly you're choosing to reintroduce.

Summary

  • Problem: A wide table duplicates facts, causing update, insertion, and deletion anomalies.
    Solution: Decompose by functional dependencies; 1NF → 2NF → 3NF → BCNF each removes one kind of duplication.
  • Problem: Fully normalized schemas can be slow to read.
    Solution: Denormalize deliberately, accepting sync cost for read speed.
