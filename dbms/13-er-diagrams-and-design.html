ER Diagrams and Database Design

So far we've been handed tables and told to query them. But where do the tables come from? Someone has to decide what tables exist, what columns they hold, and how they connect.

Problem: If you open a query tool and start writing CREATE TABLE on instinct, you'll rebuild every flaw from the file-based chapter — the same fact stored in three places, the same update applied to two of them. Bad structure is cheap to fix on paper and brutally expensive to fix once a million rows depend on it.

Solution: Model the problem before you build it. An Entity-Relationship (ER) diagram describes the real world as entities and the relationships between them, independent of any table syntax. You design it once, argue about it cheaply, then mechanically translate it into tables.

Entities and attributes

An entity is a thing worth storing — a Player, a Team, a Tournament. An attribute is a property of that thing. Attributes come in a few flavours, and the flavour decides how it maps to a column later.

Attribute typeMeaningExample
SimpleOne indivisible valuescore
CompositeSplits into partsfull_name → first, last
DerivedComputed from others, not storedage from date_of_birth
MultivaluedHolds many valuesa player's past_teams
KeyUniquely identifies the entityplayer_id

Relationships and cardinality

A relationship links entities — a player belongs to a team, a team plays in a tournament. Cardinality answers "how many of each side?"

CardinalityReads asExample
1 : 1one to onea team has one captain
1 : None to manya team has many players
M : Nmany to manyteams play in many tournaments, tournaments host many teams

Getting cardinality right is the whole game, because it decides where the foreign key goes — or whether you need a third table entirely.

Participation

Participation asks whether every entity must take part in the relationship.

  • Total: every row must participate — a player must be on a team.
  • Partial: participation is optional — a team need not be in any tournament yet.

Total participation usually becomes a NOT NULL foreign key; partial becomes a nullable one.

Weak entities

Some entities can't be identified on their own. A row in match_stats means nothing without the match and the player it belongs to — "27 kills" is only meaningful as "27 kills, by this player, in that match." A weak entity borrows part of its identity from a stronger one, and its key is a composite of its own partial key plus the owner's key.

From ER diagram to tables

The translation is mechanical once the model is right. These rules are the bridge between the whiteboard and CREATE TABLE.

In the ER modelBecomes in tables
Strong entityA table, its key as PRIMARY KEY
1 : N relationshipA foreign key on the "many" side
M : N relationshipA new junction table holding both keys as a composite key
Multivalued attributeA separate table, one row per value
Composite attributeOne column per part
Weak entityA table whose key includes the owner's key

A worked model

Take the esports domain. The entities and how they relate:

RelationshipCardinality
Player belongs to TeamN : 1
Team plays in TournamentM : N

Applying the rules gives:

  • players with a team_id foreign key (the N:1 lands here).
  • teams and tournaments as their own tables.
  • team_tournament, a junction table of (team_id, tournament_id), for the M:N.

That junction table is the part beginners forget. A many-to-many relationship can never be a single foreign key — there is no "many" side to put it on — so it always becomes its own table.

Notation

Two notations dominate. Chen draws entities as rectangles, relationships as diamonds, attributes as ovals — good for teaching. Crow's Foot draws the cardinality as little branching lines at the ends of a connector — what you'll actually see in tools like dbdiagram and most real schemas. They say the same thing; pick whichever your team reads faster.

Summary

  • Problem: Designing tables by instinct rebuilds the redundancy and anomalies of file systems.
    Solution: Model entities and relationships first; translate to tables by fixed rules.
  • Problem: A many-to-many relationship has no side to hold a foreign key.
    Solution: It becomes a junction table with a composite key.

A clean model removes redundancy by hand. The next chapter makes that process formal and provable.
