Cleanup and blind spots

The report is written, but the engagement is not done until you have cleaned up after yourself and been honest about what you could not see.

Cleanup and retest

Cleanup means removing what you left behind: test accounts, uploaded files, temporary keys, payload markers, callbacks, and scan artifacts. Retesting later confirms the fixes actually work and avoids the classic "we fixed it by moving it to another endpoint" outcome. A clean engagement ends with: findings delivered, evidence tidy, cleanup complete, fixes tracked, and a retest planned.

Name the blind spots

Black-box reporting carries one duty that white-box does not: document what you could not see. Absence of a finding is not evidence of safety, and an honest limitations section is the difference between a useful report and a falsely reassuring one.

  • Edge / WAF interference and any rate-limit-induced gaps
  • In-scope hosts or endpoints that did not respond
  • Post-authentication surfaces you never reached
  • Anything time-boxed out — name it, don't bury it

"We found no critical issues" and "we ran out of time before testing the admin API" are very different sentences. Only one of them is honest, and the client deserves the honest one.

The whole campaign, one line

From a single rude URL to a delivered report, the path was: 

scope -> recon -> scan -> enumerate -> analyze -> validate
      -> network/web/platform checks -> report -> cleanup -> retest

Each phase fed the next. If you tracked it with the campaign tracker, the timeline and artifacts are already sitting there and half the report writes itself — but the thinking, at every step, was yours.
