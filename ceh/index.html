Ethical hacking or Pentesting is the art of finding vulnerabilities in a live system and reporting the exploits to the respective organisation as part of the contract or as a participant in the bug bounty program hosted by the organisation (white-hat), for monetary or reputation purposes. The second last part matters a lot. If you find vulnerabilities and don't report, it's a grey moral zone. Adding to this, if you find bugs outside of the scope described in bug bounty program, it won't be fruitful, for you. Usually, withholding such information is individual's loss, as organisations do pay handsomely if the hole and proposed fix is legit. However, if you disclose the discovery to malicious third party or act maliciously (black-hat) using the obtained information, you're eligible to be in jail. Between those two extremes sit the grey-hats: they hack without permission — which is still illegal — but without a black-hat's malice, often disclosing the flaw afterwards (sometimes only after asking for a fee). Good intentions don't make unauthorised access legal, and that's what keeps them grey rather than white.

On the other hand, large organisations often operate this process of pentesting and defence using various teams.

Types of teams:

  • Red team - offense. Team responsible for finding holes and reporting to Blue team.
  • Blue team - defence. Team responsible for fixing these holes as reported by Red team.
  • Purple team - all rounders/hybrid. Team responsible for assisting both teams for immediate fixes.

Types of participants:

  • Black hats - Criminals. E.g., Kevin Mitnick.
    Common activities
    • Ransomware
    • Phishing
    • Credential theft
    • Malware deployment
    • Selling stolen databases
  • White hats - Professionals. E.g., Bug hunters, Red team, Consultant.
  • Grey hats - Hack without authorisation but without malicious intent, and usually disclose what they find afterwards (sometimes for a fee). E.g., an uninvited bug-finder who reports the hole later. (A disgruntled employee acting maliciously isn't a grey-hat — that's a black-hat insider by another name.)
  • State-Sponsored / APT Groups. E.g. Lazarus Group.
    Common activities
    • Disruption of enemy's national infrastructure
    • Spying, and Surveillance
    • Selling stolen databases
  • Red hats - Vigilantes.
  • Hacktivists - Individuals bent on exposing scandals and thwarting dictatorship. E.g., Anonymous.
    Common activities
    • DDoS attacks
    • Leaks
    • Protest campaigns
  • Script Kiddie / Green hat - Beginners / Amateurs - Us.

Adding to the above, there are 3 types of penetration testing (pentesting) — classified not by the hacker's intent but by how much the tester is told up front:

  • White-box - the tester gets full internal knowledge: source code, architecture, credentials.
  • Black-box - the tester is given nothing, simulating an outside attacker working from scratch.
  • Grey-box - the tester gets partial knowledge, e.g., a normal user account, simulating a logged-in user or an insider.

Note: these are "box", not "hat". A hat describes the hacker's intent and legality; a box describes how much the tester knows going in. Mixing the two up is a common slip.
