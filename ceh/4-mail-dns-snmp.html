Mail, DNS, and SNMP

Beyond SMB and AD, a handful of everyday services leak more than they should. These are reachable from outside more often than the internal ones, which makes them prime black-box targets.

SMTP — mail servers (25)

Old mail servers answer VRFY and EXPN, which confirm whether a username exists — a quiet way to validate accounts: 

$ nc 10.10.10.20 25
220 mail.lab.example.com ESMTP Postfix
VRFY jsmith
252 2.0.0 jsmith
VRFY nonexistent
550 5.1.1 <nonexistent>: Recipient address rejected: User unknown

Two different responses = user enumeration. Also note the banner (software and version) and test whether the server is an open relay — a real misconfiguration finding.

DNS — zone transfers (53)

A misconfigured name server will hand over its entire zone to anyone who asks. That is the jackpot: every record, every subdomain, in one request: 

$ dig axfr lab.example.com @ns1.lab.example.com

lab.example.com.      IN  SOA  ns1.lab.example.com. ...
lab.example.com.      IN  MX   10 mail.lab.example.com.
shop.lab.example.com. IN  A    10.10.10.5
vpn.lab.example.com.  IN  A    10.10.10.7
dev.lab.example.com.  IN  A    10.10.10.9

Most servers refuse the transfer — and a refusal is a result too, worth one line in the notes. When it works, it is the fastest subdomain list you will ever get.

SNMP — network devices (161/udp)

SNMP uses "community strings" as passwords, and far too many devices still ship with public. Guess it and the device narrates itself — interfaces, routes, running software, sometimes credentials: 

$ onesixtyone -c community.txt 10.10.10.30    # find the community string
10.10.10.30 [public] Linux fileserver 5.4.0

$ snmpwalk -v2c -c public 10.10.10.30
SNMPv2-MIB::sysDescr.0 = STRING: Linux fileserver 5.4.0-generic
IF-MIB::ifDescr.2 = STRING: eth0

NFS and FTP

Two more quick wins. showmount -e <host> lists NFS exports — look for world-readable or writable paths and exposed backups. And FTP still allows anonymous login on plenty of hosts: ftp <host>, user anonymous, and see what is sitting there.
