Hats and teams

Difficulty: Easy

"Hat" is shorthand for a hacker's intent and legality. The colour tells you whether someone has permission and what they plan to do with what they find.

Hat Who they are Typical activity
Black hat Criminals. E.g. Kevin Mitnick. Ransomware, phishing, credential theft, malware, selling stolen databases.
White hat Authorised professionals: bug hunters, red teamers, consultants. Permission-based testing and responsible disclosure.
Grey hat Hack without authorisation, usually disclose afterwards. Unauthorised but non-malicious poking. Still illegal.
Red hat Vigilantes. Strike back at attackers, often outside the law.
State-sponsored / APT Groups backed by governments. E.g. Lazarus Group. Espionage, surveillance, disruption, data theft.
Hacktivist Political or social cause-driven. E.g. Anonymous. DDoS, leaks, protest campaigns.
Script kiddie / green hat Beginners and amateurs. Running tools they do not fully understand. Us, before coffee.

One clarification that trips people up: a disgruntled employee acting maliciously is not a grey hat. That is a black-hat insider by another name. The disclosure-afterwards habit is what keeps grey hats grey; malice removes it.

Teams

Inside an organisation, the same work is split into coloured teams — here the colour describes a role, not legality.

  • Red team — offence. Finds weaknesses and reports them.
  • Blue team — defence. Detects, responds, and fixes.
  • Purple team — collaboration mode. Helps red and blue learn from the same evidence.
