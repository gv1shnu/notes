Social engineering

Difficulty: Easy

Social engineering tests people and process instead of code, so written approval matters even more here than elsewhere. You are running an exercise on humans, and it can cause real distress if handled carelessly.

Define it before you run it

A sanctioned phishing or pretext test needs its boundaries agreed in advance:

  • Targets and the pretext (the story you will use)
  • Allowed channels (email only? phone? in person?)
  • A stop word, and who can call the whole thing off
  • Data-collection limits — what you record, and what you must not
  • A debrief plan so participants learn rather than just feel tricked

The one rule that captures the risk: if HR learns about your phishing test from panicked employees before the blue team does, your test plan has become the incident. Coordinate, keep a channel open, and never improvise unsanctioned phishing from a notes repo.

What it proves

Done well, it measures how the organisation responds: how many report the email, how fast the blue team reacts, whether a click leads anywhere. Those are process findings — "reporting worked but took 40 minutes" — not a scoreboard of who fell for it.
