Reconnaissance

Reconnaissance, also called footprinting, is the first working phase. The goal is to gather as much relevant information as possible about the target and its approved scope. "Relevant" means useful for the next phase — personnel, domains, infrastructure, technologies, exposed services, business context. The website says "About us." The tester reads "about our tech stack, hiring plans, DNS habits, and forgotten subdomains."

Passive vs active

Recon splits into two modes, and the difference is simply whether you touch the target.

Passive Active
Contact None — you read public and third-party sources Direct — you query the target's own systems
Detection Effectively invisible Can be logged and noticed
Examples Search engines, certificate logs, public DNS, Netcraft DNS interrogation, light service probing

Always start passive and exhaust it before going active. Passive work costs the target nothing and tells you where to point the noisy tools later. Active recon touches the target, so it must be explicitly allowed by the rules of engagement.

What to collect

  • Owner information
  • Domains and subdomains
  • IP addresses and hosting clues
  • Employee names and email patterns
  • Technologies, third-party services, CDNs
  • Physical locations, company news and events

Every recon item should answer one question: why does this help the next phase? If the answer is "because I am nosy," delete it and drink water. And avoid surveilling individuals to find personal weaknesses — that is not pentesting, it is creepy and possibly criminal. Stay professional and in scope.

The output of this whole chapter is a target inventory: domains, subdomains, IPs, technologies, useful contacts, source links, and a note on what to scan next.
