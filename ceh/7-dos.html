Denial of service

Denial-of-service concepts are important to understand, but actually running DoS against production is one of the riskiest things in this whole set. It is the clearest "never automate, never assume" action — irreversible, and from the outside you cannot see how much you are breaking.

Prefer the safe answer

Unless the contract specifically includes availability testing, you usually answer DoS questions without launching one:

  • Architecture review — are there rate limits, autoscaling, and upstream DDoS protection?
  • Rate-limit checks — does the app throttle expensive endpoints?
  • Capacity discussion — what happens under a traffic spike, on paper?
  • Controlled lab reproduction — demonstrate the weakness on a copy, never on prod.

When availability testing is in scope, it happens in a window everyone agreed to, with a rollback plan and someone watching the target's health. A crashed production service is not a finding — it is an outage you caused.

The kinds worth knowing

Volumetric (flood the pipe), protocol (exhaust connection tables, e.g. SYN floods), and application-layer (one expensive request repeated until the app falls over). The application-layer ones are the most relevant to a web test, because a single un-throttled, costly endpoint is a design finding you can report from a rate-limit check alone.
