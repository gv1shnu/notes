SMB and Active Directory

In Windows-heavy networks, SMB (file sharing, port 445) and Active Directory (identity, via LDAP and Kerberos) are the map. Enumerating them well often decides the whole engagement, because they expose users, shares, and the relationships an attacker walks to reach admin.

SMB shares and users

A null (anonymous) session sometimes reveals shares and accounts without any credentials. enum4linux-ng (the maintained rewrite of enum4linux) rolls the common checks into one: 

$ enum4linux-ng -A 10.10.10.12

[+] Domain: LAB   Domain SID: S-1-5-21-...
[+] Shares:
      backups     READ, WRITE
      IPC$        (no access)
[+] Users:
      administrator, jsmith, svc_sql, guest

A writable backups share and a service account (svc_sql) are both leads. List and read a share directly with smbclient, or sweep many hosts with NetExec (the current tool; it replaced CrackMapExec): 

$ smbclient -L //10.10.10.12 -N          # list shares, no password
$ nxc smb 10.10.10.12 -u '' -p '' --shares
SMB  10.10.10.12  445  DC01  backups  READ,WRITE
SMB  10.10.10.12  445  DC01  IPC$

LDAP and the directory

LDAP (389) is the directory's query language. Even an anonymous bind can leak the naming context and, sometimes, the whole user list: 

$ ldapsearch -x -H ldap://10.10.10.12 -b "" -s base namingContexts
namingContexts: DC=lab,DC=local

The AD mindset

Active Directory is not a list of users; it is a graph of who can act on whom. What you are hunting for:

  • Domain and account naming patterns
  • Password policy and lockout thresholds (before you ever test a password)
  • Service accounts and their SPNs (Kerberoasting targets)
  • Group memberships, especially excessive or nested ones
  • SMB signing and trust relationships

BloodHound collects this graph and finds the shortest path from a low-privilege account to Domain Admin — the relationships a human would take hours to trace by hand. Collecting the data is read-only; walking the path is exploitation, and comes later.

Enumeration must not mutate the target. Null-session reads are safe; anything that writes to a share, changes a setting, or risks a lockout needs explicit approval.
