Session hijacking

After you log in, the server gives your browser a session token — usually a cookie — that proves who you are on every later request. Steal or reuse that token and you are that user, no password needed. Session security is mostly about protecting the cookie, and three flags do most of the protecting.

The three cookie flags

Toggle the flags and see which attacks each one closes. The cookie starts wide open.

Flag Closes
HttpOnlyJavaScript can't read the cookie, so an XSS can't steal it.
SecureCookie is only sent over HTTPS, so it can't be sniffed on plain HTTP.
SameSiteCookie isn't sent on cross-site requests, blocking CSRF.

Other session weaknesses

Beyond the flags, look for predictable tokens, session fixation (the token doesn't change after login), tokens that still work after logout, over-long idle timeouts, and tokens leaking in URLs or logs. A session that outlives its own logout is a skeleton key with a timer.
