Port scanning

Host discovery told us a machine is alive. Port scanning asks the next question: which doors are open? A port is a numbered door on a host — 22 is usually SSH, 80 is usually a web server, 443 is HTTPS. Scanning knocks on each door and listens for how it answers.

You do not knock by hand. One command does the whole building: 

$ sudo nmap -sS -T4 --top-ports 1000 10.10.10.5

Starting Nmap 7.94 ( https://nmap.org )
Nmap scan report for 10.10.10.5
Host is up (0.021s latency).
Not shown: 996 closed tcp ports (reset)
PORT     STATE    SERVICE
22/tcp   open     ssh
80/tcp   open     http
443/tcp  open     https
3306/tcp filtered mysql

Nmap done: 1 IP address (1 host up) scanned in 4.11 seconds

Four words in that STATE column carry the whole meaning. Before we trust them, we need to know what they are and how nmap decided.

The three states

Every port lands in one of these. The trap for beginners is treating "closed" and "filtered" as the same thing — they are not.

State What nmap saw What it means
open The port answered and accepted the connection A service is listening here. This is what you want.
closed The port answered, but refused The host is reachable; nothing is listening on this port right now.
filtered The port said nothing at all A firewall dropped the probe. You cannot tell open from closed — the door is behind a wall.

"Closed" means the host is talking to you and this particular port is empty. "Filtered" means something is actively hiding the port from you. One is an empty room; the other is a locked corridor you cannot see down.

How a SYN scan decides

The default scan (-sS, the "SYN scan") sends a single SYN packet — the first step of a TCP handshake — and reads the reply. It never finishes the handshake, which is why it is fast and quiet. The reply alone is enough to classify the port. Step through it:

The pattern is the whole lesson: SYN/ACK back → open, RST back → closed, silence → filtered. Because nmap answers the open port's SYN/ACK with a RST instead of an ACK, the connection is never completed, so many applications never even log it. That is the "half-open" trick, and it is why -sS is the sensible default on an authorised test.

Connect scan vs SYN scan

A SYN scan needs raw-socket privileges (hence sudo). Without root, nmap falls back to a connect scan (-sT), which completes the full three-way handshake using the operating system's normal connect call.

SYN scan -sS Connect scan -sT
Handshake Half-open (SYN, reply, RST) Full (SYN, SYN/ACK, ACK)
Needs root Yes No
Noise Often unlogged by the app Logged as a real connection
Speed Faster Slower

Same verdicts, different footprint. If you are not root, or a proxy is in the way, -sT is what you get.

UDP scanning

Everything above is TCP. UDP has no handshake — you send a packet and often hear nothing back whether the port is open or firewalled. That ambiguity shows up honestly in the output: 

$ sudo nmap -sU --top-ports 20 10.10.10.5

PORT    STATE         SERVICE
53/udp  open          domain
123/udp open|filtered ntp
161/udp open|filtered snmp

open|filtered is nmap admitting "I could not tell." UDP scans are slow (the scanner waits on timeouts) and easy to misread, so they are usually run against a small list of ports that matter — DNS (53), SNMP (161), NTP (123) — rather than all 65535. Skipping UDP entirely is how people miss an exposed SNMP service.

Timing and load

Scanning is traffic you are pointing at someone else's machine, so pace matters. The -T flag picks a template from 0 (paranoid) to 5 (insane); --min-rate sets a packet floor.

Flag Effect When
-T4 Fast, reasonable default on a healthy network Most authorised lab and internal scans
-T2 / -T1 Slow, gentle, harder to notice Fragile targets, or when you want to stay quiet
-T5 Very aggressive — can overwhelm or miss results Rarely; fast disposable labs only

Old devices — printers, cameras, industrial gear — can fall over under an aggressive scan. When in doubt, slow down. A crashed target is both a bad result and an awkward phone call.

Raw data vs interpretation

Keep two columns in your notes: what the scanner literally saw, and what you think it means. They are not the same, and mixing them turns a soft guess into a loud claim in the report.

Raw Interpretation
443/tcp open Web app candidate — check TLS, headers, auth, tech stack next.
3306/tcp filtered MySQL may exist but is firewalled from here. Note it; do not claim it is exploitable.
161/udp open|filtered Possible SNMP. Worth a targeted follow-up, not a finding yet.

A port number is only a hint anyway. The next step, service and version detection, turns "80 is open" into "nginx 1.18 serving a login page" — that is where the real leads come from.
