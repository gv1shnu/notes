Introduction

Ethical hacking, or penetration testing, is the practice of finding vulnerabilities in a live system and reporting the evidence and fix ideas to the organisation that gave you permission to look. That permission is the whole difference between a job and a crime. It usually comes from one of three places: an employment contract, a consulting engagement, or a bug bounty program.

The one rule that sits above every technique on these pages: no scope, no poke. Your terminal is not a magic wand; it is a receipt printer for consequences. If a target is not in writing as something you are allowed to test, you do not test it.

Reporting is half the job

Finding a vulnerability is only useful if you report it. If you find something and stay silent, you have entered a grey moral zone and helped no one. If you go outside the scope of a bug bounty, the work usually will not pay off anyway. Withholding a real, in-scope finding is normally your own loss too — organisations often pay well when the finding is real and comes with a reasonable fix.

The line is sharp on the other side. If you disclose the discovery to a malicious third party, or act maliciously using what you found, that is black-hat activity and can put you in serious legal trouble.

The grey area

Between the two extremes sit grey hats: people who hack without permission, often without malice, and disclose the flaw afterwards. Good intentions do not make unauthorised access legal — which is exactly why they stay grey instead of white. Typing a command you barely understand is normal and fine. Typing it on someone else's system without permission is where the story turns into a court episode.
