People and usernames

People are part of the attack surface — not to pry into their private lives, but because employees leak professional facts that matter: email formats, usernames, job titles, and the technologies they mention. Those feed later phases (email patterns become spray lists; a job posting reveals the tech stack).

Username checks — Sherlock

Sherlock takes a username and checks a long static list of sites for a matching profile URL: 

$ sherlock johndoe
[*] Checking username johndoe on:
[+] GitHub: https://github.com/johndoe
[+] Instagram: https://instagram.com/johndoe
[+] Reddit: https://reddit.com/user/johndoe
[+] Twitter: https://twitter.com/johndoe
[*] Search completed with 4 results

One username often ties together several accounts, which confirms naming habits and sometimes reveals a personal GitHub with work-related code in it.

Email and name patterns

Most organisations use one predictable email format. Confirm it once from any public address and you can generate the rest from a staff list: 

first.last@company.com     ->  jane.doe@company.com
flast@company.com          ->  jdoe@company.com
first@company.com          ->  jane@company.com

A confirmed pattern plus employee names from LinkedIn or a conference page gives you a set of likely valid accounts — useful later, and handled carefully, because credential testing against real accounts is governed by the rules of engagement.

What to collect (and what not to)

  • Email format and known addresses
  • Usernames and handles that recur across sites
  • Roles and reporting lines (who would plausibly hold access)
  • Tech mentioned in job posts, talks, and public repos

Stop at professional, relevant, publicly available facts. Digging for personal weaknesses to pressure someone is not recon; it is harassment with extra steps. If a fact does not help a later phase, it does not belong in your notes.
