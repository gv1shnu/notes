Choosing Hardware

Difficulty: Beginner

One of the biggest misconceptions about homelabs is that they require expensive enterprise servers. In reality, the best hardware is the hardware that satisfies your requirements while remaining affordable, reliable and efficient.

Start With Your Goal

Choose hardware based on what you intend to run.

GoalSuggested Hardware
Learning LinuxOld laptop or desktop.
Docker servicesMini PC.
Virtual machinesModern desktop with 32 GB+ RAM.
Media serverCPU with hardware video encoding support.
Large storageDesktop with multiple drive bays.

CPU

Virtualization and containers benefit from multiple CPU cores. Hardware virtualization extensions (Intel VT-x / AMD-V) should be enabled in firmware when available.

Memory (RAM)

RAM is usually exhausted before CPU resources in a growing homelab.

  • 8 GB – Learning.
  • 16 GB – Small homelab.
  • 32 GB – Comfortable virtualization.
  • 64 GB+ – Multiple virtual machines and services.

Storage

Separate the operating system from large data whenever possible.

SSD
 └── Operating System
 └── Virtual Machines

HDD
 └── Media
 └── Backups
 └── Archives

Networking

A reliable Gigabit Ethernet connection is recommended for servers. Wi-Fi is suitable for experimentation but wired networking generally offers lower latency and greater stability.

Power & Noise

Remember that a homelab may run continuously.

  • Lower power usage reduces operating cost.
  • Quiet systems are easier to live with.
  • Good airflow extends component lifespan.

Avoid purchasing hardware for future requirements that may never arise. Upgrade when your workload demands it.

Key Takeaway

The best homelab hardware is the system that meets your current needs while leaving room to grow. Begin with modest equipment, learn the fundamentals and expand your infrastructure as your experience and requirements increase.
