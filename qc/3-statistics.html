Statistics

High-school territory, and the course admits it — but quantum measurement is all about spreads of outcomes, so the vocabulary earns its place. The centre first:

Term Meaning
Mean the average — sum ÷ count
Median the middle value of the sorted list
Min / Max smallest / largest

Median is the honest one: a single billionaire wandering into a list of salaries yanks the mean upward, while the median barely flinches. Outliers lie to averages.

The problem: how spread out is the data?

Knowing the mean is 43.57 tells you nothing about whether the numbers huddle together or sprawl from 0 to a billion. We need a measure of spread. Naive idea: average each value's distance from the mean. Naive failure: the negatives and positives cancel to ~0. Useless.

The fix: square first

Step What
1. Variance average of squared distances from the mean (squaring kills the sign)
2. Standard deviation √variance — back in the original units, so it's actually interpretable

For the course's sample list: variance ≈ 515, so standard deviation ≈ 22. The variance (515) looks absurd next to a max of 79 because it lives in "squared units"; taking the root drags it back to a number you can read as "typical distance from the mean is about 22."

Why it matters: that exact move — square the thing, sum, take the root — is how a qubit turns its amplitudes into probabilities (the squared magnitude, coming next). Same reflex, fancier setting.
