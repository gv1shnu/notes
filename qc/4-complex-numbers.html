Complex Numbers

Difficulty: Medium

Quantum amplitudes aren't ordinary numbers — they're complex. The whole thing rests on one cheerfully impossible definition: i = √−1. There's no real number whose square is −1, so we imagined one (hence "imaginary"), and it turns out to be wildly useful.

A complex number is a real part plus an imaginary part: a + bi. For example √−9 = √9 · √−1 = 3i.

Arithmetic

Operation Rule
Add / subtract real with real, imaginary with imaginary
Multiply expand normally, then replace i² with −1
Divide multiply top and bottom by the denominator's conjugate (below)

That single substitution i² = −1 is the entire trick. Multiplying (5 + 3i)(3 + 2i) just means watch for an i² mid-expansion and flip its sign.

The two stars of the show

Two operations dominate every quantum probability calculation:

Name Definition Example on 3 + 2i
Complex conjugate (z*) flip the sign of the imaginary part only 3 − 2i
Squared magnitude (|z|²) z × z* = a² + b² (always a real number) 3² + 2² = 13

Note the magic: a complex number times its own conjugate, (3 + 2i)(3 − 2i), has its imaginary terms cancel (−6i + 6i) and lands on a clean real 13. That's also why division works — multiplying by the conjugate forces the denominator real, so you can split it cleanly.

Why it matters: a qubit's state carries complex amplitudes, and the probability of measuring an outcome is the squared magnitude of its amplitude. The conjugate is how we'll "undo" amplitudes when taking inner products. Memorize these two; the rest is bookkeeping.
