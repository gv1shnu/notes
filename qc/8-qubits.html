Qubits

Toolkit assembled, payoff time. A qubit's state is a 2-row vector — two numbers (amplitudes). The top one governs the chance of measuring 0, the bottom one the chance of 1.

State Vector [top; bottom] Meaning
|0⟩ (classical 0, "spin down") [1; 0] measure 0 with 100% probability
|1⟩ (classical 1, "spin up") [0; 1] measure 1 with 100% probability
superposition [1/√2 ; 1/√2] 50% / 50% — genuinely undecided

The rule: squared magnitudes sum to 1

Given amplitudes a (top) and b (bottom), the probabilities are their squared magnitudes, and they must obey:

|a|² + |b|² = 1

Which is just probability's "it'll be something" rule wearing a lab coat. Check the superposition state: (1/√2)² + (1/√2)² = ½ + ½ = 1. Two amplitudes that look modest, a tidy 50/50 split. Unlike a bit, a qubit isn't stuck at 0 or 1 — it can sit anywhere on the spectrum between, as long as the squares still add to 1.

Reading a probability: the inner product

To ask "what's the chance this state measures as that one?", take the inner product: the adjoint (†) of one vector times the other. For perfectly aligned states it returns 1 (certainty); for opposed states, 0. It's the same square-and-sum reflex from statistics, now deciding measurement odds — and the bra-ket notation a couple of pages on exists purely to make this less painful to write.

The catch: you only ever see a collapsed 0 or 1. The rich in-between lives entirely before measurement — which is exactly what the next page is about.
