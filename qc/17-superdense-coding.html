Superdense Coding

With gates, entanglement, and phase now in hand, we can run our first real protocol — and meet our cast: Alice and Bob (and eventually Eve, the eavesdropper) — you'll meet this trio in every quantum paper ever written. The goal: Alice sends Bob two classical bits by physically transmitting just one qubit.

The catch it has to beat

A measured qubit collapses to a single bit — one qubit "obviously" carries one bit. Superdense coding sneaks past that by spending entanglement set up ahead of time: a source entangles a pair (the usual H + CNOT) and hands one half to Alice, one to Bob, before any message exists.

Alice encodes with one gate

Alice picks a gate for her half depending on the two bits she wants to send, then ships that single qubit to Bob:

Bits to send Alice applies
00 I (identity — do nothing)
01 X (flip)
10 Z (phase)
11 X then Z (flip + phase)

Because the pair is entangled, poking her qubit reaches into the joint state. Bob — now holding both halves — runs the entangling recipe in reverse (CNOT then H) and measures, recovering both bits exactly.

Why it matters: the bits ride in which gate Alice chose, decoded by interference, not in the lone qubit's raw value. It's the first taste of "entanglement is a resource you spend." Flip the whole idea around — send a qubit's state using classical bits — and you get teleportation. Next.
