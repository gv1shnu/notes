Quantum Gates

Course 1 gave us qubits sitting in state vectors. This course makes them do something. The problem: how do you change a qubit's state? The answer is exactly what linear transformations promised — a gate is a unitary matrix, and "applying a gate" is multiplying it by the state vector. Unitary means reversible, so every gate can be undone (measurement is the lone exception).

Single-qubit gates

Gate Does Note
X flips |0⟩ ↔ |1⟩ the quantum NOT gate
Y flip + phase (carries an imaginary i) X, Y, Z are the Pauli gates
Z leaves |0⟩, flips the sign of |1⟩
H (Hadamard) |0⟩ → equal blend of |0⟩ and |1⟩ creates superposition — the workhorse

Don't memorize the matrices — recognize the moves. X is just the NOT gate from page one, now a 2×2 matrix: multiply it by |1⟩ and out pops |0⟩. Z famously does nothing to |0⟩ (its −1 has nothing to bite on), which trips up everyone exactly once.

Hadamard: the superposition machine

Apply H to |0⟩ and you get the 50/50 superposition (1/√2)(|0⟩ + |1⟩) — written |+⟩. Apply it to |1⟩ and you get (1/√2)(|0⟩ − |1⟩) — written |−⟩. Both are still 50/50 on measurement; the difference is a phase (the sign), which matters more than it looks. Almost every algorithm opens by Hadamard-ing everything into superposition, then steering it with interference.

Why it matters: Qiskit ships dozens of gates, but once you see them as matrices multiplying a vector, a gate you've never met is just a multiplication you haven't done yet. The interesting power, though, starts when one qubit controls another — next page.
