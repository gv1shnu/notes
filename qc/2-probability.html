Probability

Measuring a qubit doesn't return "the answer" — it returns an answer, with some probability. So before any quantum mechanics, we need the grammar of chance. P(A) is the probability of event A, a number from 0 (never) to 1 (always). Everything else is about combining events.

Two questions: AND and OR

AND = both happen. OR = at least one happens. How you compute them depends on how the events relate:

Relationship Meaning Rule
Mutually exclusive can't both happen — P(A AND B) = 0 P(A OR B) = P(A) + P(B)
Independent one says nothing about the other P(A AND B) = P(A) × P(B)
Independent (OR) add, then remove the double-counted overlap P(A OR B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A AND B)

Say A = I go out tonight and B = my wife goes out tonight. If someone must stay home with the kid, A and B are mutually exclusive. If a babysitter shows up, they become independent — and the dog, frankly, has its own plans.

Worked example

P(A) = 30%, P(B) = 40%, independent. Then:

Quantity Computation Result
P(A AND B) 0.30 × 0.40 0.12
P(A OR B) 0.30 + 0.40 − 0.12 0.58

Forget to subtract the overlap and you'd claim 70% — counting the "both go out" world twice. That subtraction is the same bookkeeping that keeps quantum probabilities honest.

Why it matters: a qubit's two outcomes must have probabilities that add to exactly 1 — it's going to be something. Keep that "must sum to 1" rule in your pocket; it reappears the moment we write down a qubit's state.
