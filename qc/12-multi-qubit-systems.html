Multi-Qubit Systems

Difficulty: Hard

One qubit is a 2-entry vector. The obvious question: how do two qubits combine? Not by addition — by the tensor product (⊗). And that's where the exponential magic from page one finally shows up.

Combine by tensoring

Tensor two single-qubit kets and you get a 4-entry state. The four basis states for two qubits:

Ket Built from Vector
|00⟩ |0⟩ ⊗ |0⟩ [1; 0; 0; 0]
|01⟩ |0⟩ ⊗ |1⟩ [0; 1; 0; 0]
|10⟩ |1⟩ ⊗ |0⟩ [0; 0; 1; 0]
|11⟩ |1⟩ ⊗ |1⟩ [0; 0; 0; 1]

Each entry is the probability amplitude for one combination. And the count explodes:

Qubits Amplitudes
1 2
2 4
10 1024
n 2n

This is the "all 256 at once" promise from the introduction, now with receipts: n qubits carry 2n amplitudes simultaneously. 300 qubits hold more amplitudes than there are atoms in the observable universe. (Storing that on your laptop: not recommended.)

The grand finale: the Bell state

Put it all together in one state:

(1/√2)( |00⟩ + |11⟩ )

Outcome Probability
00 50%
01 0%
10 0%
11 50%

Read it off: it's a 50/50 superposition — and it's entangled, because the only outcomes are 00 and 11. Measure the first qubit, get 0, and the second is instantly 0 too; never a mismatch. Superposition and entanglement, living in one four-number column. That little state is the whole course in miniature.

Where this goes

You now have the foundations: the math toolkit, the three quantum principles, and the notation to write states without crying. Later courses spend this on quantum gates (those reversible unitary matrices), circuits, measurement, and the famous algorithms — Shor's, Grover's, and friends.
