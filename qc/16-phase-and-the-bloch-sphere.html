Phase & the Bloch Sphere

Back on the gates page, the Z gate did "nothing" to probabilities yet clearly did something. That something is phase — a hidden part of a qubit's state that probabilities alone can't show. To see it, we need a better picture than a bar chart.

The Bloch sphere

A single qubit's full state is a point on the surface of a sphere:

Location State
North pole |0⟩
South pole |1⟩
Equator 50/50 superpositions — |+⟩, |−⟩, and everything between

Latitude sets the measurement probabilities (how far from the poles); longitude is the phase. A gate is a rotation of this point — Hadamard, for instance, is just a particular quarter-turn. (Careful: the Bloch sphere only draws one qubit. Entangle two and it can't depict them — entanglement isn't a property either qubit holds alone.)

Phase: the equator's secret

|+⟩ and |−⟩ both measure 50/50 — identical histograms — yet they sit on opposite sides of the equator. The only difference is phase (a sign). Phase gates spin a state around that axis:

Gate Phase rotation
Z half turn (180°)
S quarter turn (90°)
T eighth turn (45°)
rx / ry / rz any custom angle you like

Why a "hidden" thing matters

Measure a qubit directly and phase is invisible. But sandwich a phase between two Hadamards — H · Z · H — and the outcome flips entirely. The second Hadamard turns the phase difference into an amplitude difference that interference can act on. That conversion (phase → interference → measurable result) is the engine of every quantum algorithm coming up: hide the answer in phases, then interfere it into the open.
