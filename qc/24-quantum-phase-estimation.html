Quantum Phase Estimation

QPE is the payoff of the QFT and the last piece before Shor. The problem: a gate U adds a phase to a state — U|ψ⟩ = e2πiθ|ψ⟩ — but you don't know θ. Estimate it.

Why you can't just measure it

A phase is invisible to a direct measurement (recall the Z gate leaving probabilities untouched). You could apply U and measure thousands of times to guess θ statistically — slow and imprecise, especially for small angles. QPE reads it out directly instead.

The circuit

Step Action
1 a counting register of n qubits, all Hadamard'd into superposition
2 apply controlled-U powers (U, U², U⁴, …) onto |ψ⟩
3 each one kicks θ back into the counting register's phases at a different rate
4 apply the inverse QFT, then measure → the bits are θ

The controlled-U powers write θ across the counting qubits as a Fourier-basis pattern; running the QFT backwards (QFT†) collapses that pattern into an ordinary binary number — θ to n bits of precision. More counting qubits, more precision. This is phase kickback grown up: instead of one bit of phase, you extract a whole high-resolution number.

Why it matters: a startling number of hard problems can be recast as "find the phase of some unitary." The biggest of them — factoring — is next, and it's QPE that does the heavy lifting.
