Quantum Fourier Transform

The QFT is the gear that makes Shor turn, but it stands on its own. It's a change of basis: it moves a state from the computational basis (is it 0 or 1?) to the Fourier basis (what's its phase pattern?). Information that was sitting in which state gets re-expressed as phases spread across the qubits.

The one-qubit case you already know

For a single qubit the QFT is just the Hadamard gate: it sends |0⟩ → |+⟩ and |1⟩ → |−⟩, swapping "computational" for "phase." The general n-qubit version is the same idea scaled up:

|x⟩ → (1/√N) Σy e2πi·xy/N |y⟩  (N = 2n)

Don't fight the formula — read the intent: it stamps the number x into a set of rotations, one per qubit, each turning at a different rate. Built from Hadamards and controlled phase rotations, with swap gates at the end to undo the reversed qubit order (the reason you'll spot stray SWAPs in QFT circuits online).

Why it's a big deal

Transform Cost
Classical FFT O(n · 2n)
QFT O(n²)

Why it matters: the QFT is exponentially cheaper than its classical cousin, and it's how a quantum computer reads periodicity — a repeating pattern hidden in a state. Run it in reverse (the adjoint, QFT†) and phases collapse back into a number you can measure. That reverse step is exactly what the next page, phase estimation, uses to extract an answer.
