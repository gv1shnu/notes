Linear Transformations

The last piece of the toolkit — and the one that quietly is quantum computing. First, a name change: a column matrix (one column, several rows) is what we'll call a vector. Not the arrow-in-physics kind — here a "vector" just means a column of numbers. A qubit's state will be exactly this.

Transform = matrix × vector

Plot a point (x₁, y₁) and you can send it somewhere new (x₂, y₂) by multiplying it by a matrix — rotating, scaling, reflecting it. That's a linear transformation. A rotation by angle θ, for instance, is a fixed matrix of sines and cosines; multiply your vector by it and it spins to a new spot.

This is the whole mechanic of a quantum gate: a qubit's state is a vector, a gate is a matrix, and "applying a gate" is just multiplying them. You'll let Python (Qiskit) grind the arithmetic — but this is what it's doing under the hood.

Two families that define everything

Both are defined purely by how the dagger X† relates to X:

Family Condition Represents
Unitary X† = X−1 reversible operations — quantum gates
Hermitian X† = X irreversible operations — measurements / observables

A unitary matrix's dagger is its inverse, so every gate can be cleanly undone — you can put a qubit into superposition and pull it back out. A Hermitian matrix equals its own dagger and models the one thing you can't undo: looking.

Why it matters: that's the entire toolkit. State = vector, gate = unitary matrix, gate application = matrix × vector, measurement = the irreversible Hermitian one. Time to spend it — on actual qubits.
