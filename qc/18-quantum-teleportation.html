Quantum Teleportation

Superdense coding sent classical bits with a qubit; teleportation does the reverse — it moves a qubit's unknown state |ψ⟩ from Alice to Bob using entanglement plus two classical bits. And no, nothing physically travels Star-Trek style; the information is what relocates.

The problem: you can't just copy a qubit

The no-cloning theorem: there is no operation that copies an unknown quantum state. It makes grim sense — you can't read the state without measuring, and measuring destroys it. So "copy |ψ⟩ to Bob" is off the table from the start. Teleportation moves it instead, leaving nothing behind.

The protocol

Step Who Action
1 Source entangle a pair; give one qubit to Alice, one to Bob
2 Alice CNOT from |ψ⟩ onto her half, then H on |ψ⟩
3 Alice measure her two qubits → two classical bits (|ψ⟩ is now destroyed)
4 Alice send those two bits to Bob (ordinary channel)
5 Bob apply corrections based on the bits → his qubit becomes |ψ⟩

Alice's measurement leaves Bob's qubit in one of four versions of |ψ⟩ (flipped, phase-shifted, or both). Her two bits tell him which, and he undoes it — no if statements needed, just a controlled-X and controlled-Z keyed off the bits:

Alice's bits Bob applies
00 nothing
01 X
10 Z
11 Z then X

Two things that save you from sci-fi nonsense: the original is measured away, so no-cloning is respected (it's a move, not a copy); and Bob is stuck until Alice's classical bits arrive, so nothing beats light speed. Entanglement spent + 2 classical bits = one qubit's state, relocated.
