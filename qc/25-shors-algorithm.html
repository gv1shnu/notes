Shor's Algorithm

Difficulty: Hard

The headliner. Shor's algorithm factors large numbers exponentially faster than any known classical method — and that quietly threatens most of the encryption running the internet.

The problem: factoring is hard (on purpose)

RSA rests on one asymmetry: multiplying two big primes is easy, but recovering them from the product is brutally hard. A 256-bit number would take a classical computer thousands of years to factor — which is precisely why your card payments are safe today.

Factoring Cost
Classical (best known) exponential — millennia for big keys
Shor (quantum) polynomial — minutes, given a big enough machine

The trick: factoring is secretly period-finding

Shor's leap is realizing you don't attack the factors head-on. Instead:

Step Action Where
1 pick a random a < N classical
2 find the period r of ax mod N (it repeats every r) quantum
3 if r is even, gcd(ar/2 ± 1, N) gives a real factor classical

The sequence a, a², a³, … mod N cycles with some period r; once you know r, a little modular arithmetic and a GCD pop the factors out. Step 2 — finding r — is the only hard part, and it's exactly phase estimation: the period hides as a phase of the "multiply by a" operation, and QFT + QPE read it out where interference makes the period's multiples stand out and everything else cancel.

The fine print: it's probabilistic — a random a gives a usable (even) period only about half the time, so you may retry. And in practice you'll just call a library; the genius is in why it works, not the typing.

Why it matters: a large, reliable quantum computer running Shor breaks RSA — which is why the world is busy inventing post-quantum cryptography before that day arrives. The threat is real; the timeline is the open question. Which brings us to the hardware.
