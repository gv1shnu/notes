Quantum Hardware

Every algorithm so far assumed perfect qubits. Reality begs to differ. The closing question of the course: what are these qubits actually made of, and why don't we all have a quantum computer yet?

The enemy: decoherence

A qubit's quantum behaviour is fragile — a stray bit of heat, light, or vibration and the superposition collapses, taking your information with it. Coherence time is how long a qubit holds its state before that happens. Today's qubits are noisy and short-lived, which is why machines hover around 50–100 usable qubits, not the thousands Shor would need to scare anyone's bank.

The contenders

Technology Idea Players Trade-off
Superconducting tiny currents on a chip IBM, Google, Rigetti fast, familiar to make — but needs near absolute-zero fridges
Photonic states of light (photons) Xanadu, PsiQuantum room temperature — but the optics get big fast
Ion traps charged atoms held in fields IonQ, Honeywell long coherence — but slower and hard to scale
Annealers special-purpose, not reprogrammable D-Wave thousands of qubits — but only for one kind of problem

(Microsoft is chasing a fifth path — topological qubits — betting on hardware that's intrinsically noise-resistant. We'll see.) No winner has emerged; superconducting is merely the current front-runner.

Where this leaves us: we're in the noisy, small-scale era — the algorithms work, the machines just aren't big or clean enough yet. Closing that gap is the job of error correction (a "to add" topic for a later course). You now have the full arc: the math, the qubits, the gates and circuits, the protocols, and the algorithms that make the whole enterprise worth the refrigeration bill.
