Matrices

Difficulty: Medium

If quantum computing had a national sport, it would be matrix multiplication. A matrix is just a table of numbers (real or complex), described as rows × columns — a 2×3 matrix has 2 rows, 3 columns. Always rows first; the course will not let you forget this.

The easy operations

Operation How
Add / subtract element by corresponding element (same shape required)
Scalar multiply multiply every element by the number

Matrix × matrix — the one that matters

Each result entry is a row of the first matrix dotted with a column of the second: multiply the lined-up numbers, add them up. Two rules fall out of this:

Question Answer
Can A × B even happen? only if columns(A) = rows(B)
What shape is the result? rows(A) × columns(B)
Is A×B = B×A? no — order matters (often B×A isn't even legal)

That last point — matrix multiplication is not commutative — is not a technicality. In quantum computing it's the reason the order you apply operations changes the outcome.

Tensor product — not multiplication

Different beast, different symbol (⊗). The tensor product doesn't shrink things down to a dot-product; it blows them up, multiplying every element of the first matrix by the whole second matrix and stacking the results. A 2-element vector ⊗ a 2-element vector gives a 4-element one.

Why it matters: a single qubit is a 2-row column matrix. Two qubits aren't "added" — they're tensored, giving 4 entries; three give 8; ten give 1024. That exponential blow-up is precisely where a quantum computer's power (and your future headaches) live. We'll cash this in at multi-qubit systems.
