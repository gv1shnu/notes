Superposition & Interference

Difficulty: Medium

The previous page wrote superposition down as a vector. This page is the physics that earns it — the double-slit experiment (Young, ~150 years before anyone said "qubit"). Shine light through a barrier with two slits and watch what hits the screen.

The puzzle

Setup You'd expect You get
One slit one band of light one band ✓
Two slits two bands many bands — an interference pattern

Waves explain that easily: ripples from each slit overlap, reinforcing in some places (constructive) and cancelling in others (destructive). So light is a wave. Case closed. Except quantum mechanics never lets a case stay closed.

Wavefronts spread from each slit and overlap. Where crests meet crests they reinforce (bright bands); where crest meets trough they cancel (dark gaps). The striped screen — not two clean bands — is the fingerprint of interference, and it survives even when photons arrive one at a time.

The weird part

What they tried What happened
Add a detector to see which slit each photon takes pattern vanishes — two bands, like particles
Unplug the detector (leave it sitting there) pattern returns
Fire photons one at a time pattern still builds up

The only explanation that survives

A lone photon, with nothing else around, interferes with itself. It went through the left slit and the right slit — not as two photons, but as one photon holding both possibilities at once. That is superposition: not "in many places," but carrying the possibility of every place until something forces the issue.

And the act of measuring is what forces it: the moment anything records which slit the photon took, the interference is gone. The textbook story calls this collapsing the wave function — though whether anything physically "collapses," or that's just our bookkeeping catching up, is a matter of interpretation rather than settled fact. The photons don't "know" they're watched in any spooky sense — but the maths is merciless about it, and yes, this is the part where Feynman reminds you that you don't understand it.

The three pillars: superposition (hold all options), interference (let amplitudes add and cancel to steer the result), and — next page — entanglement.
