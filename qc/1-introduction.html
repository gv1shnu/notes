Introduction

A classical computer sees the entire world — this sentence, your playlist, every cat video — as nothing but 1s and 0s. A transistor either has voltage (1) or doesn't (0). Stack those bits and you can encode any number, letter, colour, or frame of video. Genuinely revolutionary… and also the source of the one limitation quantum computing exists to attack.

The problem: one number at a time

With n bits you can represent any of 2n values — but only one of them at any instant. Eight bits hold some number from 0 to 255; to try all 256 you must run through them one…by…one.

The idea: certainty is overrated

A qubit, thanks to superposition, holds 0 and 1 simultaneously — not as a stored value, but as a probability of being either until you look. Wire several together with entanglement and let their possibilities interfere, and the machine can explore an exponential space of answers in one sweep. That is the whole pitch: for a specific class of problems, an enormous jump in what's computable.

A fair warning before the hype runs away with us: this is not magic, and it does not make everything faster. As Feynman put it, "if you think you understand quantum mechanics, you don't understand quantum mechanics." Comforting, isn't it.

What this course actually is

To speak qubit, you first need the language: a little probability, some complex numbers, and a lot of matrices. This first course builds that toolkit, then cashes it in on qubits, superposition, and entanglement. Gates, circuits, and the famous algorithms come in later courses — foundations first.
