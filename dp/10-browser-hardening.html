Browser Hardening & Safe Browsing

Difficulty: Medium

Your web browser is one of the most exposed applications on your computer. Almost every online activity—banking, shopping, email, research and social media— passes through it. Hardening your browser reduces unnecessary data exposure and limits opportunities for attackers.

Browser hardening does not mean making the browser anonymous. It means configuring it to minimise tracking, reduce attack surface and avoid unsafe behaviour.

Every enabled feature increases functionality, but also increases the amount of information available to websites and the number of components that could contain security vulnerabilities.

Keep Your Browser Updated

Modern browsers receive frequent security updates. Many attacks rely on exploiting known vulnerabilities that have already been patched.

  • Enable automatic updates.
  • Restart the browser after updates.
  • Avoid unsupported browser versions.

Browser Extensions

Extensions can improve productivity, but they execute with significant privileges inside the browser.

Good Practice Avoid
Install only necessary extensions. Installing dozens of extensions "just in case".
Review requested permissions. Granting access to every website without checking.
Remove unused extensions. Leaving abandoned extensions installed.

Cookies & Site Data

Cookies are useful for login sessions and user preferences, but they also enable tracking across browsing sessions.

Consider periodically clearing site data for websites that do not require long-term sessions. Logging out of services when they are no longer needed also reduces persistent tracking.

Site Permissions

Modern browsers ask permission before granting access to sensitive resources. Only approve permissions that are genuinely required.

PermissionExample Risk
LocationReveals approximate physical location.
CameraVideo capture.
MicrophoneAudio capture.
NotificationsSpam or misleading alerts.
ClipboardAccess to copied information.

Downloads

Treat downloaded files as untrusted until proven otherwise.

  • Download software from official sources whenever possible.
  • Be cautious with executable files.
  • Verify file hashes when publishers provide them.
  • Avoid disabling browser security warnings.

Phishing

Many successful attacks do not exploit software vulnerabilities—they exploit people.

Before entering credentials, check:

  • Is the domain name correct?
  • Was the link expected?
  • Does the page request unusual information?
  • Is there unnecessary urgency or pressure?

Browser Profiles

Separate browser profiles help isolate different activities. 

Browser

├── Personal
├── Work
├── Banking
└── Research

Each profile maintains separate cookies, history, saved logins and settings, reducing accidental information leakage between activities.

Key Takeaway

A hardened browser reduces unnecessary exposure but does not replace good security practices. Keeping software updated, limiting extensions, reviewing permissions and recognising phishing attempts together provide a much stronger defence than relying on a single privacy feature.
