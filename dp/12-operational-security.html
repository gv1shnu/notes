Operational Security (OPSEC)

Difficulty: Hard

Technology alone cannot guarantee privacy. Many successful attacks exploit human behaviour rather than software vulnerabilities. Operational Security (OPSEC) is the practice of reducing the amount of useful information you reveal through your daily actions and habits.

Good OPSEC is about thinking before sharing, understanding who might observe your activities, and making deliberate decisions based on your threat model.

What is OPSEC?

Originally developed for military and intelligence operations, Operational Security is now widely used in cybersecurity to identify and reduce information leakage.

Many small pieces of information may appear harmless individually, but together they can reveal far more than intended.

Oversharing

Every photo, social media post or public profile contributes to your digital footprint. Ask yourself whether the information is necessary before publishing it.

Information Potential Risk
Boarding Pass Travel details and booking information.
Work Badge Employer identification.
Vacation Photos Indicate that your home may be unoccupied.
Desktop Screenshot Expose open applications, usernames or documents.
Background Objects Reveal addresses, family details or sensitive paperwork.

Common Mistake: Focusing only on the subject of a photo while ignoring everything visible in the background.

Social Engineering

Social engineering manipulates people into revealing information or performing actions they normally would not.

Examples include phishing emails, fake technical support calls, fraudulent QR codes and impersonation on messaging platforms.

  • Verify unexpected requests.
  • Question urgency.
  • Never share authentication codes.
  • Confirm identities through an independent channel.

Public Wi-Fi

Public networks should be treated as untrusted environments.

  • Prefer HTTPS websites.
  • Avoid accessing sensitive accounts on unfamiliar networks unless necessary.
  • Disable automatic connection to open Wi-Fi networks.
  • Turn off file sharing when using public networks.

Physical Security

Privacy does not end at the keyboard.

  • Lock your device before leaving it unattended.
  • Use a privacy screen if working in crowded environments.
  • Keep recovery codes in a secure location.
  • Protect storage devices and backup media.

USB Devices

Unknown USB devices should never be trusted automatically.

A malicious USB device may behave as a keyboard, network adapter or storage device and execute unexpected actions within seconds of being connected.

Only use USB devices obtained from trusted sources.

Travel Considerations

When travelling:

  • Carry only the devices you need.
  • Keep software updated before departure.
  • Be cautious when charging devices using unknown USB ports.
  • Review what data is synchronised to the device.

Key Takeaway

Many privacy incidents result from ordinary habits rather than sophisticated attacks. Developing good operational security means making privacy-conscious decisions every day, both online and offline.
