Browser Tracking & Fingerprinting

Every time you visit a website, your browser exchanges information with the web server. Some of this information is required to display the page correctly. Other information is used to recognise returning visitors, measure engagement, personalise content, or build advertising profiles.

Understanding how browsers are identified is essential because many modern tracking techniques work even when you never create an account.

The browser sends information such as the requested URL, IP address, browser version, operating system, language preferences and many HTTP headers. The server responds with the web page and may also instruct the browser to store additional information.

Cookies

A cookie is a small piece of data stored by your browser on behalf of a website. The browser automatically sends matching cookies back whenever you revisit that site.

Cookies themselves are not malicious. They allow websites to remember logins, shopping carts and user preferences. Problems arise when cookies are used to track behaviour across many different websites.

Browser Fingerprinting

Suppose cookies are disabled. Can websites still recognise the same browser? Often, yes.

Browser fingerprinting identifies devices by combining dozens of seemingly harmless properties into a unique fingerprint.

  • Operating system
  • Browser version
  • Screen resolution
  • Time zone
  • Language
  • Installed fonts
  • Graphics hardware
  • Canvas rendering
  • WebGL support
  • Audio processing characteristics

Individually these values are common. Combined together they often become highly unique.

Unique Browser Profile

Unlike cookies, browser fingerprints cannot simply be deleted because they are generated from characteristics of the browser and device.

Other Tracking Techniques

  • Local Storage — stores larger amounts of browser data than cookies.
  • ETags — cache validation headers that may also identify returning visitors.
  • Browser Cache — previously downloaded resources reveal browsing behaviour.
  • Session Storage — temporary browser storage cleared after closing the tab.
  • Link Decoration — identifiers appended to URLs.

Can Incognito Mode Prevent Tracking?

Incognito or Private Browsing primarily prevents your own browser from storing local history after the session ends.

It does not hide your IP address, prevent browser fingerprinting, make you anonymous, or stop websites from collecting information during that session.

Key Takeaway

Modern websites rarely rely on a single tracking technique. Instead, cookies, browser fingerprints, IP addresses and other identifiers are combined to recognise visitors with greater confidence.
