Data Backups & Encryption

Difficulty: Medium

Privacy is not only about preventing unauthorized access to information. It is also about ensuring that important data remains available when hardware fails, ransomware encrypts your files, or devices are lost or stolen.

Backups and encryption solve different problems. A backup protects against data loss, while encryption protects against unauthorized access.

Backups vs Encryption

Backup Encryption
Protects availability. Protects confidentiality.
Recover files after accidental deletion. Prevent others from reading your files.
Useful after hardware failure or ransomware. Useful if a device is lost or stolen.

Full Disk Encryption

Full disk encryption encrypts every file stored on a drive. Without the correct credentials, the contents remain unreadable even if the storage device is removed from the computer.

Most modern operating systems provide full disk encryption features. Once enabled, encryption works transparently after successful authentication.

VeraCrypt

The built-in options (BitLocker on Windows, FileVault on macOS) encrypt the whole system drive but are tied to that operating system. VeraCrypt — the actively maintained successor to TrueCrypt — is cross-platform and open-source, and adds a capability the built-in tools lack: container encryption.

Its specialities:

  • Encrypted containers. VeraCrypt creates a single file that behaves like a virtual encrypted disk. Mount it with the password and it appears as an ordinary drive; unmount it and it is once again an opaque, meaningless blob. You can carry that container on a USB stick or store it in the cloud, and it opens on Windows, macOS or Linux.
  • Vendor-independent full-volume encryption. It can also encrypt entire partitions or system drives without relying on the OS vendor — useful when you cannot use, or would rather not trust, the built-in option.
  • Hidden volumes. A second encrypted volume can be concealed inside the first, so that revealing one password need not reveal that the other even exists — a form of plausible deniability.

The catch: VeraCrypt protects data at rest only. While a container is mounted, its contents are exactly as exposed as any other open file, so unmount it when you are done. And, as with all such tools, there is no recovery key — lose the password and the data is gone.

Cloud Storage

Cloud storage improves availability by synchronizing files across multiple devices. However, synchronization is not the same as backup.

If an unwanted change immediately synchronizes to every device, you may still need version history or separate backups to recover the original file.

There is a second problem with cloud storage: the provider — and anyone who compromises it — can read whatever you upload. Full disk encryption does not help here, because the files are decrypted the moment they leave your drive for the cloud.

Cryptomator

Cryptomator closes that gap by encrypting files on your device before they ever reach the cloud folder. It creates a vault inside your Dropbox, Google Drive or OneDrive folder; you unlock it locally and work with your files normally, while what actually syncs to the provider is per-file ciphertext with encrypted filenames.

Its specialities:

  • Transparent, per-file encryption. Each file is encrypted individually rather than inside one large container, so the cloud can still sync only the files that changed. You keep efficient incremental sync while the provider sees nothing readable.
  • Provider-agnostic and open-source. It works with any service that syncs a folder, and — like Bitwarden and VeraCrypt — its code is public and auditable.
  • Zero-knowledge. The password never leaves your device; the provider stores only encrypted blobs.

VeraCrypt or Cryptomator? They solve neighbouring problems and are easy to confuse:

VeraCrypt Cryptomator
Built for A single portable encrypted container Cloud sync of many small files
Best when Encrypting local drives or removable media Keeping a cloud provider from reading your files
Structure One large volume file Many individually encrypted files

Recovery Keys

Many encryption systems generate recovery keys during setup. These keys allow access to encrypted data if the normal authentication method becomes unavailable.

Store recovery keys securely and separately from the encrypted device. Do not save the only recovery key on the same encrypted drive.

Ransomware

Ransomware encrypts files and demands payment for the decryption key. A reliable backup is usually the most effective recovery strategy.

  • Keep offline or disconnected backups.
  • Regularly test backup restoration.
  • Do not assume backups are working until restoration has been verified.

What Should You Back Up?

  • Personal documents.
  • Photos and videos.
  • Password manager database.
  • Important configuration files.
  • Recovery keys.
  • Personal projects.

Applications can usually be reinstalled. Personal data is often irreplaceable.

Key Takeaway

Encryption protects data from unauthorized access, while backups protect data from loss. Both are necessary. An encrypted drive without backups can still lose data permanently, and unencrypted backups expose sensitive information if they fall into the wrong hands.
