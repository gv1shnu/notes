Digital Privacy — Introduction

Difficulty: Easy

Every action performed online leaves traces. Visiting a website, installing an application, sending a message, purchasing a product, or simply carrying a smartphone generates information about you. Some of this information is necessary for a service to function; much of it exists because collecting data has become an integral part of the modern internet.

When people first explore this subject, three terms appear repeatedly: privacy, security, and anonymity. They are closely related, but they describe different objectives. Confusing them often leads to unrealistic expectations and poor decisions.

Privacy

Privacy is the ability to control what information about you is collected, shared, stored, and disclosed.

Who knows what about me?

Improving privacy means reducing unnecessary data collection, limiting tracking, and deciding who should have access to your personal information.

Security

Security protects systems and information from unauthorised access or modification.

Can someone access my information without permission?

Strong passwords, encryption, and multi-factor authentication improve security, but they do not automatically improve privacy.

Anonymity

Anonymity prevents actions from being linked back to your identity.

Can someone determine that this activity belongs to me?

Removing your name alone rarely makes you anonymous. IP addresses, browser fingerprints, cookies, payment methods and behavioural patterns can all be correlated.

Threat Model

Before choosing any privacy tool, define your threat model. It describes what you want to protect, who you want to protect it from, and what happens if that information becomes known.

Asset
    ↓
What do I want to protect?

Adversary
    ↓
Who am I protecting it from?

Capability
    ↓
What can they do?

Impact
    ↓
What happens if they succeed?

A student avoiding advertising trackers, a journalist protecting confidential sources, and a malware analyst all have different threat models. The correct solution depends on the problem being solved.

A Layered Perspective

Privacy is not achieved by installing one application. It is the result of many independent decisions.

Privacy
    ↓
Control information disclosure

Security
    ↓
Protect information

Anonymity
    ↓
Reduce identity linkage

The remainder of this course focuses on understanding how tracking works, how identities are correlated, and how to choose appropriate techniques based on your own threat model.
