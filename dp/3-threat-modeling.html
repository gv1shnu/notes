Threat Modeling

Privacy advice without context is often misleading. Before choosing a browser, VPN, password manager, or anonymous communication platform, you must first understand what problem you are trying to solve.

This process is known as threat modeling. Rather than asking "What is the best privacy tool?", ask "What am I protecting, who am I protecting it from, and what happens if I fail?"

What is a Threat Model?

A threat model is a structured way of identifying risks before choosing a solution. Every security and privacy decision should be based on a clearly defined threat model.

Assets

An asset is anything valuable that you want to protect. Assets are not limited to files or passwords.

  • Personal identity
  • Passwords
  • Financial information
  • Private conversations
  • Photos and documents
  • Location history
  • Professional reputation
  • Research data

Adversaries

An adversary is any person or organisation that may collect, access or misuse your information.

  • Cybercriminals
  • Advertising companies
  • Data brokers
  • Scammers
  • Malware authors
  • Employers or schools (depending on context)
  • Curious acquaintances

Not every adversary has the same capabilities. A phishing scam and a sophisticated state-sponsored attacker operate very differently.

Capability

Capabilities describe what an adversary can realistically do.

CapabilityExample
LowGuess weak passwords or search social media.
MediumSend phishing emails or deploy commodity malware.
HighPurchase leaked databases, exploit vulnerabilities, perform targeted attacks.

Overestimating capabilities leads to unnecessary complexity. Underestimating them creates unnecessary risk.

Impact

Finally, evaluate the consequences if your information is compromised.

  • Minor inconvenience
  • Financial loss
  • Identity theft
  • Reputational damage
  • Loss of confidential data
  • Personal safety

The higher the impact, the more effort should be invested in protecting the asset.

Examples

Person Primary Concern Likely Threat Model
Student Advertising trackers Reduce data collection and improve account security.
Software Developer Source code and credentials Protect repositories, passwords and development systems.
Journalist Confidential sources Protect identities, communications and metadata.
Business Owner Customer data Prevent breaches and ransomware.

Key Takeaway

There is no universally "best" privacy setup. A solution that is appropriate for one person may be excessive or insufficient for another. Understanding your own threat model allows you to make informed decisions instead of blindly following online recommendations.
